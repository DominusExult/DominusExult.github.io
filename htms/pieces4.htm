THE TIMES WEEKEND * Saturday August 8 1998 * feature * Page 21
By David Bowker
David Bowker was told by a fan to get a life, and enjoy it. But this week´s challenge, to be happy, was too much. Only stupid people are happy
Recently, a Mr. K. Childs from Cheshire wrote to me, pointing out that I never stop moaning. He said: "Although I enjoy reading about your weekly misadventures, I hope for your sake that your real life isn´t as miserable as the life you depict in your column." As a postscript Mr. Childs added: "Are you interested in Doctor Who by any chance?"
Actually, Mar Childs, my life is purgatory. Not that it´s any of your business. Since my childhood, numerous people have criticized me for being unduly negative. The fact that these critics have included my friends and family, my psychiatrist and everyone else who knows me, leads me to suspect that they may have a point.
It isn´t that I want to be miserable. I would willingly enjoy life if other people weren´t always ruining it for me. Take your letter, for example. It´s always nice to receive fan mail. I was cheered by the fact that you liked my column. Then I read the comment about Doctor Who and realized you were a nutter. Fan letters from nutters don´t count. But at least this particular nutter gave me the idea for this week´s challenge: seven days trying to enjoy life.
On the enjoyment front, last Saturday looked promising. I went to a wedding. Unlike most couples, the bride and groom liked each other. There was a good chance that they would be together for at least three years before they divorced. The ceremony was simple but moving. The bride´s veil all but covered her five o´clock shadow and the groom had remembered to put his false teeth in.
When the vicar pronounced the couple husband and wife, they almost looked happy. But I found myself thinking about how, in the face of eternity, our earthly joys are as fleeting as a single beat of a butterfly´s wings. Life is cruelly short. How long before the happy couple keeled over and died? If they were going to go, I reflected, maybe it would be better for them to die now, while they had the church booked. That way, the vicar could perform two ceremonies for the price of one.
The wedding and the reception passed without fatalities. I eliminated the risk of food poisoning by not eating. Instead, I consumed several glasses of beer. In no time at all, I was feeling decidedly sanguine.
Then I noticed the father of the bride. His name is Colin. He´s a 55-year-old company director who drives a Porsche Boxster, which should tell you all you need to know. Colin was on the dance floor, shaking his creaky pelvis at a 17-year-old bridesmaid. Now Colin is to dancing what Ghandi was to bodybuilding. Despite his lack of coordination, he was having a marvelous time. This ruined my day. There is nothing I hate more than the sight of stupid people enjoying themselves.
I didn´t try to enjoy myself on Sunday, the most depressing day of the week. But on Monday, I went to a lovely restaurant in West London. I ordered an expensive meal and was just about to enjoy myself when the film director Michael Winner sat down at the next table. Needless to say, the mere sight of him put me off my food so I had to leave.
On Thursday, I made a last-ditch attempt to celebrate being alive. My wife and I spent the night in a luxurious hotel. We drank champagne and dined in the hotel´s award-winning restaurant. At 11 o`clock we went to bed, only to be awoken two hours later by the sound of two people enjoying themselves. Worse still, they sounded like stupid people. Adding insult to injury, they seemed to be enjoying themselves in our wardrobe. I opened the wardrobe door to investigate. Then I realized that the noise was coming from the next door. Our stupid neighbors continued to enjoy themselves stupidly until stupid daybreak.
By Friday, I was too tired to care about this column. I gave up trying to enjoy myself. I poured myself a stiff drink and locked myself in my room and sulked all day long. Funnily enough, I had the time of my life.
